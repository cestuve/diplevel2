Alex: The Invisible Beginning

Alex was 15 when his mom first took him for a diabetes screening.It didn’t feel urgent.He was healthy and enjoyed soccer practice after school, late-night snacks, and joking with friends.

Scroll to begin

The only reason they were there was family history.

His uncle had type 1 diabetes.

“Just a blood test,” his mom said.

Clinical pause

If Alex has no symptoms, why screen?

What should you tell Alex? Select all that apply.

Alex has a family history of type 1 diabetes. Although he is not exhibiting symptoms, stage 1 and stage 2 are usually presymptomatic, and screening can identify his risk.
The result

The results came back quietly.

Autoantibodies

No symptoms
No changes
Nothing Alex could feel

But something had already begun.

The immune system had already started attacking his beta cells.

Once autoantibodies appear, the immune system has begun targeting insulin-producing beta cells. From that point forward, progression to clinical diabetes becomes increasingly likely over time.

1

Genetic risk

Family history or known genetic risk raises concern.

2

3

Autoantibodies

IA, GAD, IA-2, or ZnT8 may be detected.

4

Beta-cell loss

Insulin production declines over time.

Months passed. Then a year.

Alex still felt normal, but his body was changing in ways no one could see.

Stage Alex

Alex has two or more islet autoantibodies.

His glucose is normal. Given ≥2 autoantibodies and normoglycemia, what stage is Alex in?

Stage 1: at least 2 autoantibodies, normoglycemia, and presymptomatic
Stage 2: at least 2 autoantibodies, dysglycemia, and typically presymptomatic
Stage 3: at least 2 autoantibodies, overt hyperglycemia, and symptomatic clinical onset

Having autoantibodies chnaged Alex’s future risk.

The younger a person is, the faster progression from Stage 1 to Stage 3 may occur.

Clinical meaningRisk is not abstract. It shapes monitoring, education, referral, and planning.

His doctor explained it gently.

“This doesn’t happen overnight. It’s been developing for a while.”

Alex nodded, but it didn’t feel real.

How could something so serious feel like nothing at all?

A new result

Alex still feels well, but his glucose pattern has changed.

Follow-up testing now shows dysglycemia. What stage is Alex in now?

Dysglycemia moves Alex into Stage 2 T1D. This is a critical referral moment, because Stage 2 identification enables consideration of immune-modulating therapy.

Beta-cell function is declining.

Type 1 diabetes develops gradually through a predictable decline in beta-cell function.

1

Slow process

Develops over months to years.

2

Starts before symptoms

Autoimmunity begins long before diagnosis.

3

Diagnosis happens late

Symptoms appear after significant beta-cell loss.

4

C-peptide declines

Insulin production falls as beta-cell mass declines.

Referral moment

What should primary care do now?

Alex is not alone in this moment. His care team has a role to play. Select all the actions that belong in the primary care role.

Primary care identifies increased risk, educates, refers for autoantibody screening and staging, coordinates care, and reinforces follow-up.

Delay does not mean cure, but delay can matter.

For individuals with Stage 2 T1D, teplizumab may be considered after specialty confirmation.

Communication choice

Alex needs clarity, not alarm.

Choose the explanation most likely to support understanding and follow-through.

Clear language, dialogue, shared decision-making, and scheduled follow-up help families understand what is happening without panic.

Later, sitting in the clinic room, Alex stared at his hands.

“So, this didn’t just happen?”

His doctor shook her head.

“It’s been progressing for years. The difference is that we caught it earlier than most people do.”

Why early detection matters

Hyperglycemia can affect the same body systems.

Once hyperglycemia is established, both T1D and T2D pathways share downstream metabolic and vascular complications.

And that mattered.

Because now, Alex and his family understood something powerful:

  • The disease didn’t start with symptoms.
  • It started long before with silent autoimmunity.
  • Early detection gave them time to prepare, learn, and act.

That night, Alex still felt like himself.

Because he was.

But now he knew something most people don’t:

Even when you feel fine, your body can be telling a story.
And if you listen early enough, you can change how that story unfolds.
